## Meet Claude

Let me be straight with you: there are a lot of AI assistants out there right now. ChatGPT, Gemini, Copilot, Grok — you've probably tried at least one. So why are we spending an entire course on Claude?

Because Claude isn't just another chatbot with a different name. It's built differently — from the ground up — in ways that matter a lot once you start using it for real work. Not "write me a poem" work, but "help me debug this authentication flow at 11 PM before tomorrow's demo" work.

Claude is a family of large language models (LLMs) built by **Anthropic**, an AI safety company founded in 2021 by former members of OpenAI. It's designed around three core properties: **helpful, harmless, and honest**. Those aren't just marketing words — they're engineering constraints that shape how the model is trained, how it responds, and where it draws the line.

Here's what that looks like in practice. I once asked Claude to help me implement a JWT authentication flow. Instead of just dumping boilerplate code, it flagged that my token expiration was set to 30 days (too long for most apps), suggested I add refresh token rotation, and warned me about a subtle timing attack vulnerability in my comparison function. A less safety-conscious model would have happily generated the insecure version and moved on.

## Who Is Anthropic?

Anthropic was founded by **Dario Amodei** (CEO) and **Daniela Amodei** (President), along with several other researchers who were deeply involved in developing earlier large language models — including GPT-2 and GPT-3 at OpenAI. They left because they wanted to build AI with safety as the *core* mission, not a side project.

What makes Anthropic different from most AI labs:

- **Safety research is the product, not an afterthought.** They publish research on interpretability (understanding what's happening inside neural networks), alignment (making sure AI does what humans actually want), and evaluation (measuring whether safety measures actually work).
- **They put their money where their mouth is.** Anthropic has published papers on topics like "sleeper agents" — studying how AI systems could potentially hide deceptive behavior — not because they want to build deceptive AI, but because understanding the risk is the first step to preventing it.
- **They're transparent about limitations.** Anthropic regularly publishes model cards and system prompts, showing exactly how Claude is instructed to behave. That kind of transparency is rare.

This matters to you as a user because the company's priorities directly shape the tool's behavior. An AI lab that prioritizes engagement metrics builds a chatbot that tells you what you want to hear. An AI lab that prioritizes safety and honesty builds a tool that tells you what you *need* to hear — including "I'm not sure about this" and "that approach has a security issue."

## Constitutional AI: Claude's Secret Sauce

Here's where Claude gets genuinely interesting from a technical perspective.

Most AI assistants are trained with a technique called **RLHF** (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback). The basic idea: humans rate AI outputs as good or bad, and the model learns from those ratings. It works, but it has a problem — it's expensive, slow, and limited by the humans doing the rating.

Anthropic developed something called **Constitutional AI (CAI)**, and it's a fundamentally different approach:

1. They give Claude a set of principles — a literal "constitution" — that covers things like helpfulness, honesty, and harm avoidance
2. Claude generates a response
3. Claude then **critiques its own response** against those principles
4. Claude revises the response based on its own critique
5. This self-improvement loop trains into the model over time

Why does this matter to you? Because it means Claude's safety behavior isn't a filter bolted on top — it's woven into how the model thinks. When Claude declines to help with something problematic, it's not because a keyword filter caught a bad word. It's because the model genuinely evaluated the request against its principles and decided the response could cause harm.

The practical upside: Claude is better at nuance. It won't refuse to discuss security vulnerabilities in the context of fixing them. It won't panic when you ask it about encryption. It understands *intent* and *context*, not just keywords. That makes it dramatically more useful for real engineering work, where half the job is handling sensitive topics responsibly.

## What Makes Claude Different in Practice?

Let me get specific, because vague claims about AI being "better" are useless.

### Long Context Windows

Claude can process up to **200,000 tokens** in a single context window. To put that in perspective:

- A typical novel is about 70,000-100,000 tokens
- A medium-sized codebase (50-100 files) fits comfortably
- An entire API specification with all its endpoints, schemas, and examples? Usually no problem

I use this constantly. Last week I needed to understand a legacy Node.js API with zero documentation. I loaded the entire `src/` directory — about 40 files — into a Claude Code session, and asked it to map out the architecture, identify the main request flow, and document the undocumented endpoints. It produced a comprehensive overview in under a minute that would have taken me half a day to write by reading the code manually.

Other models choke on large inputs. They either hit token limits and silently truncate your content (meaning they're working with incomplete information and you don't know it), or they lose coherence over long contexts — the infamous "lost in the middle" problem where information in the middle of a large input gets effectively ignored. Claude handles long contexts more reliably.

### Instruction Following

This is Claude's superpower for Claude Code specifically. Claude is *exceptionally* good at following detailed, structured instructions. When you give it a CLAUDE.md file with your project conventions, coding style, preferred libraries, and architecture decisions, it actually follows them. Consistently.

Try this: tell Claude "always use `const` instead of `let` unless the variable is reassigned, use early returns instead of nested if-else blocks, and name boolean variables with `is`/`has`/`should` prefixes." Then ask it to write ten different functions. It will follow those conventions in every single one. That consistency is what makes it a reliable development tool rather than a toy you have to babysit.

### Honest About Uncertainty

This is the one that saves you the most time, and you won't appreciate it until it happens.

Other models will confidently generate code using an API that doesn't exist, or reference a library function with the wrong signature, or describe a configuration option that was deprecated two years ago. They'll do this with the same confident tone as when they're telling you something correct. That's a *hallucination*, and it's the most dangerous failure mode for a coding assistant.

Claude is trained to be upfront when it's uncertain. You'll see responses like:

- "I'm not sure if this API endpoint accepts that parameter — you should check the docs"
- "This worked in version 3.x, but I'm not certain if the syntax changed in version 4"
- "I can write this, but I should note that I'm not 100% confident about the edge case handling here"

That kind of honesty is invaluable. It means when Claude *is* confident, you can generally trust it. And when it flags uncertainty, you know exactly where to double-check.

### Safety-First, Not Safety-Only

One thing that trips people up: they hear "safety-focused" and assume Claude is going to refuse half their requests or wrap everything in disclaimers. That's not how it works.

Claude is remarkably good at understanding context. Ask it to "write code that scans for open ports" and it'll help you — because port scanning is a legitimate security and networking tool. Ask it to "write code that brute-forces SSH passwords on systems I don't own" and it'll decline — because that's unauthorized access.

The line is drawn at intent and context, not keywords. This is Constitutional AI in action. It makes Claude feel like a colleague with good judgment rather than a paranoid hall monitor.

## Claude's Model Family

Anthropic offers several versions of Claude, each optimized for different use cases. Think of it like choosing between a sports car, a sedan, and a scooter — they're all vehicles, but you pick the right one for the trip.

### Claude Opus — The Heavy Hitter

**Best for**: Complex reasoning, architecture decisions, difficult debugging, code review, large refactors

Opus is the most capable model in the family. When you're staring at a gnarly race condition in your concurrent code, or you need to design a database schema for a complex domain, or you're trying to understand why your distributed system loses messages under load — Opus is what you reach for.

The tradeoff: it's slower and costs more. Don't use Opus to rename a variable.

### Claude Sonnet — The Daily Driver

**Best for**: Most everyday tasks, feature implementation, writing tests, explaining code, general Q&A

Sonnet is the balanced option and the default model in Claude Code. It's fast enough to not feel sluggish, capable enough for 80% of tasks, and cost-effective enough to use all day. If you're not sure which model to pick, Sonnet is the right answer.

### Claude Haiku — The Speed Demon

**Best for**: Quick tasks, boilerplate generation, formatting, simple transformations, high-volume work

Haiku is blazingly fast and dirt cheap. When you need to generate 20 similar test files, convert a bunch of TypeScript interfaces, or add documentation comments to every function in a file — Haiku gets it done in seconds. Don't waste Opus tokens on work that Haiku handles perfectly.

**Pro tip**: In Claude Code, you can switch models mid-session with the `/model` command. Start a complex debugging session with Opus, then switch to Haiku once you've identified the fix and just need to apply it across multiple files. This is one of the easiest ways to manage your token spend.

## How You'll Use Claude in This Course

Throughout this course, you'll work with Claude primarily through **Claude Code**, Anthropic's official command-line interface. Claude Code gives you direct access to Claude's capabilities right from your terminal — it can read your files, write code, run commands, manage Git operations, and much more.

If you've used ChatGPT or the Claude web interface, Claude Code is a different experience. Instead of copying and pasting code back and forth between a browser and your editor, Claude Code works directly in your project. It sees your file structure, reads your code, and writes changes directly to your files. It's the difference between asking a contractor for advice over the phone versus having them come to your workshop and work alongside you.

We'll cover installation and setup in the next lessons. For now, the important thing to understand is that everything you've learned about Claude — the long context, the instruction following, the honesty about uncertainty — becomes dramatically more useful when Claude has direct access to your codebase instead of just seeing whatever you paste into a chat window.

## Common Misconceptions

Before we move on, let me clear up a few things that confuse newcomers:

**"Claude is just ChatGPT with a different name"** — No. Different company, different training approach (Constitutional AI vs. RLHF), different strengths. Claude excels at long context processing, instruction following, and honest uncertainty. They're different tools.

**"AI safety means Claude is less useful"** — The opposite. Safety training makes Claude more reliable, not less capable. A model that confidently hallucinates fake APIs isn't "more capable" — it's more dangerous.

**"I need the most expensive model for everything"** — You don't. Match the model to the task. Haiku for simple stuff, Sonnet for most work, Opus for hard problems. Using Opus for everything is like driving a Ferrari to get groceries.

**"Claude will replace me"** — Claude is a force multiplier, not a replacement. It makes good developers faster. It doesn't make non-developers into developers. You still need to understand what you're building, make architectural decisions, and verify the output. Claude handles the tedious parts so you can focus on the interesting ones.
